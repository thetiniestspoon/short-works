---
title: His Beloved Creation
type: creative-prose
---

I find myself wondering: how might it have gone differently if Dr Frankenstein hadn't have seen his creation — the so-called "monster" — as an abomination? What if he hadn't run from it with despair and disdain?

Imagine instead: in that warm dank womb of a lab, on an electric summer night when the storm made the air vibrate and the Doctor's hair stand on end, when that cord of lightning snapped down from heaven and smacked life into one more earthly being so it could sit up with nascent sight and an unblemished mind — what if the Doctor had instead pulled it close to him and whispered:

"My Beloved! Here you are with me at last."

One must see how differently the so-called "monster" could have been seen when those strangers arrived at the castle door with nothing better to do than scream and mob about. Imagine if the doctor had stood at the threshold and said, "Put down your torches for this being — it is light and life incarnate." Maybe they would have been able to see the so-called "monster" for something more than a collection of broken parts.

Sure, some would suggest that the Doctor ought to have collected the pieces from the living, that this creation was unnatural simply for its composition. And sure, the Doctor might have gotten lucky with a finger or two donated here and there, perhaps a foot or a kidney from the more generous, but there are some parts that simply cannot be given away. Brains and hearts are inextricably woven into our beings, and simply put the doctor was not seeking to create a life of digits and other expendable bits. What was the point in all of that? And besides, is any of us truly that much more than a collection of parts held fast by a need to live and love, a need that runs through us like sutures and glue?

What if the good Doctor had leaned in close, had held his Creation's head in his hands, and kissed the scars that meandered across its gentle face. Then maybe that so-called "monster" would have fallen instead into step with the beat of a loving heart. Its days might have been spent languishing under the summer sun, seeking life in the light and shadow that dances in streams of clouds flowing across the sky. Or perhaps it might have developed a knack for composing poetry, piecing together clunky stanzas that might evoke the life it felt inside and around. Perhaps the Doctor might not have died alone on a cold sheet of ice, but rather in the warm arms of his Beloved — this creature of life and death, dark and light, who in his final moments would pull him close and whisper:

"My Beloved! Here you are with me at last."
