---
title: The Virtues of Constance
type: dark-comedy
---

The train blustered off onto the distant shadows of tracks, leaving Constance alone on the elevated platform. The air was heavy with the sticky humid of the hot August night, and already drips of sweat were matting her blouse to her back.

"Of course, he's forgotten me," she spat, looking around the empty station and sizing up what it would take to camp for the night, certain as she was that her son would not arrive until morning. She was just bending down to open her suitcase and start fashioning a makeshift bed when she was suddenly plunged under a cold and oppressive spotlight.

Her pupils tightened like nooses holding fast to a felon's neck. The bleat of the car horn shook her - only for a moment - and she marched herself to the car. The trunk popped open as she drew near.

"Not even helping his tired mother," she cursed into the dimly lit trunk. She threw in her suitcase and slammed it closed. Then with a terse huff, she threw her purse and then herself into the sunken back seat of the sedan.

"Mom, come sit up here with me." She could feel his gaze searching her face in the rearview mirror. Her knees dug into the back of her son's seat, a helpful connection to have should he doze at the wheel. A quick bump in the back could save their lives.

"I'd rather not," she retorted. Her eyes locked on the passenger dash, and she felt his weight shift in the seat. She took the opportunity to bite at him. "Did you ever go see a mechanic? I told you about that airbag recall months ago."

His squirming stopped. "How could I—"

"Exactly. How could you? You drive your family from appointment to appointment and don't think for once about the risks you're taking. Honestly, I thought I raised you better than that, Peter."

He let out a tired sigh. "Suit yourself." They drove in unrelenting silence for some time.

The car careened madly down the highway, plummeting with abandon through the dark heart of night. Its headlights, all the while, cast the endless abyss into furious contrast. Inside the circled light leading the car, the asphalt, the guardrails, the oncoming cars - all roared through in frantic blurs. Outside the circle, nothing but darkness, calm and dead, and yet she could sense the unseen guardrails on either side. They loomed closer in the darkness then lurked away, remaining hidden but pressing in tighter and tighter. Her hands gripped at whatever they could find — the door handle, then the seatbelt, then each other, falling into a sort of writhing dance in her lap.

"Peter, are you falling asleep? You're swerving like a drunk." She pushed her knees into the seat to be sure he'd heard.

"I'm fine, mom. Are you feeling carsick?"

"Nonsense! I've never once been carsick." Indeed, her stomach was a proven vice. As a girl she had once swallowed ten marbles on a dare, and none had ever come out. She felt his eyes on her again, but she couldn't look away from the fury of highway in the headlights ahead. She swore the car was swaying like they were navigating a stormy sea wrought with rolling waves set on smothering their meager ship. Her stomach turned over with seasickness, and she swallowed as the taste of marbles filled her mouth.

"Why don't you turn on the radio?" she barked. She hoped the music might keep him more alert. His hand fiddled blindly, seeking hold of the controls, and after a moment, a sudden opera pierced the cold silence of the car. A soprano with a taut voice leapt along precise arpeggios. "Such tripe," she proclaimed.

As he continued to scan the stations in silence, it occurred to her how short he was being. She resented how quickly he seemed to want this car ride to be over, even though she'd only just arrived. Judging by the way he was hurtling the car down the dark road it was increasingly apparent that he yearned for her to burn up like a meteorite falling from heaven. She felt an unshakable need to slow the car so that she and Peter would have time to properly reconnect. It had been years since her last visit, after all.

As the headlights of passing cars scanned the interior of the sedan, her eyes flit from ceiling to seat to floor. At last, she spied an umbrella, lying haphazardly atop a pile of outdated atlases. She grabbed the curved wooden handle and pulled the umbrella close, wrapped it tight with the clasp, and maneuvered it beneath Peter's seat. With it in place, she wiggled the umbrella, determining the location of its tip relative to her son's feet and the pedals. She felt unsurprised that Peter failed to notice her. She had always seen him for what he was - clueless and aloof - and she had learned long ago that it was incumbent upon her to manage the world for him, lest he fall prey to its pitfalls.

The umbrella caught hold of what she was certain was the brake pedal, so she gave a firm press, and the car slowed. Peter's brow furrowed in what might have been confusion. On the radio, a talk show host was busy explaining the way of things to his audience.

"Peter, the radio! Can't you find some music?" His hand returned to groping at the controls, and the sounds of static and fractured conversation again filled the car.

Constance soon felt the headlights of the truck behind them burning on her neck. Peter's driving was now too slow. She could see already how they were going to be rear ended by this impatient truck driver. And she knew that Peter wouldn't even see it coming. In the end, he'd slam on the brakes in a moment of clueless panic, crushing his dear mother in a great vortex of metal and blood.

She maneuvered the umbrella over to the accelerator and jammed it down hard on the pedal. The sedan lurched safely away from the truck behind them just as the car radio finally caught hold of a station.

"She had a lot on her mind, and she didn't pay attention."

Constance realized that she had been holding her breath. She took the fleeting moment of respite to catch it again, and search the rearview mirror for a modicum of awareness in her son's eyes. In the inconsistent light, she saw with painful clarity that he was nodding off at the wheel. She gasped. Then she coughed, hard and loud. Her knees pushed into his back, but he remained silent. He always had been a deep sleeper, yearning to live in worlds of dream and fantasy. She unfastened her seatbelt and leaned forward over the center console, grabbing the knob for the volume. She cranked up the music, and the country ballad abruptly filled the car to brimming.

"BEFORE SHE KNEW IT, SHE WAS SPINNING ON A THIN BLACK SHEET OF GLASS.."

"What are you doing?!" Peter cried, suddenly free from his hibernation. His hand leapt knowingly this time and returned the volume to a gentler level. His voice left a shock of vibrance that hung in the silent air. She sensed an anger that must have been pulsing beneath his pores as he practically elbowed her back into the seat. She marked the moment in her mind. She was certain to discover a bruise on her breast later that night — the very breast that had nursed him so long ago. She was nearly shocked at how unconcerned he was for her. She caught his eyes in the mirror. He was so clearly tired, near exhaustion. How fortunate he was to have such a caring mother.

"Well, let's not go on in silence. For God's sake, Peter, turn the music back on." She took the moment of distraction to again adjust the car's speed with the umbrella.

"She threw her hands up in the air. Jesus, take the wheel!"

"Jesus at the wheel? He wouldn't know the first thing about driving!" The humor seemed lost on Peter, though the comical song droned on nonetheless. After a torturous crawl through the chorus, she threw herself over the center console and turned off the music. She sat back again, nudging the umbrella with her foot to pick up the car's pace.

"I was waking you up," she explained.
