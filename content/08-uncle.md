My uncle and I were crammed in the back of the car like overstuffed luggage, and I was struggling for breath. It was a long trip from Florida to Tennessee and being crushed between my uncle and the car door had made it that much more excruciating. I only knew Uncle Mikey for that summer. As my father had driven him home from the airport, my mother had explained to me: "Don't let him touch you. Your uncle has a gay disease." It was a layered command and one that left my young mind reeling with questions, but before I could make sense of her warning, my uncle had arrived. He had a friendly demeanor, and a face like my father's but softer somehow. I was wary of him, and when it came time to pile into the car for the road trip, I was condemned to be stuck at his side.

Later that summer, I found myself at the bottom of a hotel pool,

the weight of the water compressing heavily on my chest,

my body struggling for breath, and I

relenting to the unforgiving pressure

I lay there in that cool, blue ether until I could take it no longer. My eyes opened and my arms lifted my body slowly to the surface. I swallowed the humid air with delight as my cloudy vision cleared. Uncle Mikey was nearby, doing back flips into the cobalt water, and as he surfaced alongside me, he laughed, saying he had bumped his head on the pool bottom that time. Trickles of red began to run rivulets down his face, and my eyes widened as I realized the danger to the others in the pool. I turned and swam to the edge and hoisted myself out. I had to protect these people from the gay disease, so I cupped my hands and announced to them: "This man has AIDS. Everyone exit the pool." Uncle Mikey locked himself in his room for the rest of the summer.
